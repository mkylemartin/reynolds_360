Item number 1:

<b>Famous for being famous</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> term for someone who attains <a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">celebrity</a> status for no particularly identifiable reason (as opposed to fame based on achievements, skill, or talent) and appears to generate their own fame, or someone who achieves fame through a family or relationship association with an existing celebrity.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenkins_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenkins-1">[1]</a></sup>

Item number 4:

The term originates from an analysis of the media-dominated world called <i>The Image: A Guide to Pseudo-events in America</i> (1961), by historian and social theorist <a href="/wiki/Daniel_J._Boorstin" title="Daniel J. Boorstin">Daniel J. Boorstin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards-2">[2]</a></sup> In it, he defined the celebrity as "a person who is known for his well-knownness".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He further argued that the graphic revolution in journalism and other forms of communication had severed fame from greatness, and that this severance hastened the decay of fame into mere notoriety. Over the years, the phrase has been glossed as "a celebrity is someone who is famous for being famous".<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards-2">[2]</a></sup>

Item number 5:

The British journalist <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Muggeridge" title="Malcolm Muggeridge">Malcolm Muggeridge</a> may have been the first<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to use the actual phrase in the introduction to his book <i>Muggeridge Through The Microphone</i> (1967) in which he wrote; "In the past if someone was famous or notorious, it was for something—as a writer or an actor or a criminal; for some talent or distinction or abomination. Today one is famous for being famous. People who come up to one in the street or in public places to claim recognition nearly always say: 'I've seen you on the telly!<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>

Item number 6:

<a href="/wiki/Neal_Gabler" title="Neal Gabler">Neal Gabler</a> more recently refined the definition of celebrity to distinguish those who have gained recognition for having done virtually nothing of significance — a phenomenon he dubbed the “Zsa Zsa Factor” in honor of <a href="/wiki/Zsa_Zsa_Gabor" title="Zsa Zsa Gabor">Zsa Zsa Gabor</a>, who parlayed her marriage to actor <a href="/wiki/George_Sanders" title="George Sanders">George Sanders</a> into a brief movie career and the movie career into a much more enduring celebrity.<sup id="cite_ref-Gabler_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gabler-5">[5]</a></sup> He goes on to define the celebrity as “human entertainment,” by which he means a person who provides entertainment by the very process of living.<sup id="cite_ref-Gabler_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gabler-5">[5]</a></sup>

Item number 7:

<i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/Amy_Argetsinger" title="Amy Argetsinger">Amy Argetsinger</a> coined the term <b>famesque</b> to define actors, singers, or athletes whose fame is mostly (if not entirely) due to one's physical attractiveness and/or personal life, rather than actual talent and (if any) successful career accomplishments. Argetsinger argued, "The famesque of 2009 are descended from that dawn-of-TV creation, the Famous for Being Famous. Turn on a talk show or <i><a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Squares" class="mw-redirect" title="Hollywood Squares">Hollywood Squares</a></i> and there'd be <a href="/wiki/Zsa_Zsa_Gabor" title="Zsa Zsa Gabor">Zsa Zsa Gabor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Joyce_Brothers" class="mw-redirect" title="Dr. Joyce Brothers">Dr. Joyce Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Nelson_Reilly" title="Charles Nelson Reilly">Charles Nelson Reilly</a>, so friendly and familiar and—what was it they did again?" She also used actress <a href="/wiki/Sienna_Miller" title="Sienna Miller">Sienna Miller</a> as a modern-day example; "Miller became famesque by dating <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Jude_Law" title="Jude Law">Jude Law</a> . . .</span> and then really famesque when he cheated on her with the nanny—to the point that she was the one who made <a href="/wiki/Balthazar_Getty" title="Balthazar Getty">Balthazar Getty</a> famesque (even though he's the one with the hit TV series, <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_%26_Sisters_(2006_TV_series)" title="Brothers &amp; Sisters (2006 TV series)">Brothers &amp; Sisters</a></i>) when he reportedly ran off from his wife with her for a while."<sup id="cite_ref-Argetsinger_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Argetsinger-6">[6]</a></sup>

Item number 8:

<b>Celebutante</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of the words "<a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">celebrity</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9butante" class="mw-redirect" title="Débutante">débutante</a>". The male equivalent is sometimes spelled <b>celebutant</b>. The term has been used to describe heiresses like <a href="/wiki/Paris_Hilton" title="Paris Hilton">Paris Hilton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Richie" title="Nicole Richie">Nicole Richie</a> in <a href="/wiki/Infotainment" title="Infotainment">entertainment journalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-llog_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-llog-7">[7]</a></sup> The term has been traced back to a 1939 <a href="/wiki/Walter_Winchell" title="Walter Winchell">Walter Winchell</a> society column in which he used the word to describe prominent society debutante <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Frazier" title="Brenda Frazier">Brenda Frazier</a>, who was a traditional "high-society" debutante from a noted family, but whose debut attracted an unprecedented wave of media attention.<sup id="cite_ref-llog_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-llog-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-winchell_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-winchell-8">[8]</a></sup> The word appeared again in a 1985 <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> article about <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>'s clubland celebrities, focusing on the lifestyle of <a href="/wiki/James_St._James" title="James St. James">James St. James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Edelstein" title="Lisa Edelstein">Lisa Edelstein</a> and Dianne Brill, who was crowned "Queen of the Night" by <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-llog_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-llog-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
